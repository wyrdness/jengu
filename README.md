## Overview

The Jengu (singular) or Miengu (plural) are water spirits from the oral traditions of the Sawa-speaking peoples of Cameroon's coast and river regions, particularly the Duala, Bakweri, Malimba (Limba), Subu, Bakoko, and Oroko ethnic groups. Among the Bakweri the singular form is liengu and the plural maengu. Jengu are described as beautiful, mermaid-like beings with long hair and distinctive gap-teeth. They inhabit the oceans, rivers, and lakes of Cameroon — particularly the Wouri River, its tributaries, the Wouri estuary, and nearby islands. Their spiritual functions are broad and benevolent: they act as intermediaries between the living and ancestral spirits; control the fate of humans and animals in waterways; bring good fortune to those who venerate them; cure disease through healing rituals; and provide prophetic messages about coming years. European traders and explorers documented jengu worship as well established along the Cameroonian coast by the early 19th century. The jengu are venerated through dedicated ritual specialists and community ceremonies. The annual Ngondo festival, inaugurated in 1949, is the most prominent public celebration associated with jengu veneration, bringing together Sawa peoples along the Wouri River in Douala. The jengu tradition is related to similar water-spirit beliefs across the region: the bisimbi of Kongo religion and the Mamba Muntu figure present across West and Central African cultures.
